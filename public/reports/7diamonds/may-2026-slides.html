May was a high-cadence month built around two pillars: a Mother's Day full-list push in the first week and a steady drumbeat of men's product-category drops (seersucker, linen, pants, swim, denim, polos) through the rest of the month. Strategy: what went out

Takeaways — Campaigns

What worked & what to adjust

Headlines

  • Men's drives the business. 15 men's email sends produced ~$92.6K (74% of email campaign revenue). Continue the category-drop cadence and protect this engine.
  • Best individual performers were men's bottoms and swim: Short Sleeve Shirts ($11.1K), Swim Edit 02 ($9.8K) and Generation Pants ($8.4K) led the month.
  • SMS punches above its weight. $0.23 revenue/recipient vs $0.08 for email — reserve SMS for marquee launches and time-sensitive offers.
  • Mother's Day full-list sends delivered reach and strong opens (46–47%) but low click/conversion — broad "edit" emails work better as awareness than direct-response.
  • Women's & wholesale are under-leveraged (combined ~$10.8K). Test more frequent women's category drops to the engaged female segment.
  • Low performers lagged on product & audience, not timing. Pullovers opened at 58% but barely converted — a warm layer in late-May heat while swim, shorts and pants were winning.

Patterns across all 40 sends

  • Tighter segments open ~10 points higher. Affinity / 180-day-engaged sends opened 56–63% vs 46–49% for full-list — segmentation is the biggest engagement lever.
  • Heavy cadence, healthy list. ~34 email + 6 SMS sends, yet unsubscribes held at ~0.10% and delivery at 99.6% — the disciplined exclusions protected the base.
  • Pant/denim affinity did the heavy lifting — and took the most sends. Six drops in May; performance held, but watch this segment first for fatigue.
  • Resends work as a second touch. The Seersucker resend added ~$4.7K; the Shorts AM→PM split recovered extra orders. Vary the subject on the second touch — easy win.
  • Weekends are fair game for this list. Saturday (Zuma) and the two Sunday-noon sends performed in line with weekday drops — no weekend penalty, so the calendar can flex around demand.

Takeaways — Flows

  • Welcome flows are the crown jewel. Welcome SMS ($62.8K) + Welcome Email ($39.4K) drove $102K — over half of flow revenue. Keep new-subscriber capture funded and well-supplied with offers.
  • Abandonment recovery is healthy. Add-to-Cart, Browse and Checkout combined for ~$48K; Add to Cart's $411 AOV is the highest in the account.
  • Loyalty flows are emerging winners. Loyalty Welcome ($9.5K) and Points Earned ($5.6K) convert well — expand loyalty-triggered automation.
  • Zero-revenue flows to review: Loyalty Points Reminder and Wishlist drove engagement but $0 attributed revenue — revisit offer, timing, or product links.
  • Highest-unsub flows: Cross Sell (48) and Welcome Series (175) — monitor frequency and content to protect the list.

Takeaways — Enrollment Forms

  • Forms drive the list. ~95% of May's new email subscribers came through sign-up forms — the top of the funnel worth protecting and optimizing.
  • The main mobile pop-up sets the blended rate. 73.9K impressions at a 1.7% submit rate drags the overall 2.79% down — the single biggest place a conversion lift would compound.

Creative Report

🏆 Best creative of the month

MENS – Short Sleeve Shirts · $11,060 · $0.18/recipient · 40 orders

Why this won best creative

  • A specific, benefit-clear subject. "Clean Classic Shirts" sets a plain, confident expectation — paired with a preview ("New men's short sleeves, in now.") that confirms the offer.
  • Shoppable from the first scroll. It opens on a clean single-product hero, not a lifestyle or text banner — the product is the message.
  • Right product, right segment, right time. An in-season staple sent to the engaged men's base — the month's best efficiency at $0.18/recipient and top revenue overall.

Overall, May was a strong, disciplined creative month. Consistent brand look — single-product heroes on white, steady logo lockup, tidy preview text. Performance separated on audience and specificity, not design quality: product-named men's drops won; broad Mother's Day sends drove opens but converted less efficiently. Opens are inflated by Apple Mail Privacy — the real gap is click-through.

★ Main takeaway for the team

  • The creative is doing its job — the next gain isn't prettier emails, it's sharper specificity. Name the product in the subject, lead on a shoppable hero, put the offer in the subject line, and carry the men's "name it, show it" discipline into the women's and full-list sends. Then chase the click, not the open.

Takeaways — SMS

  • Offer drove the top send. "Mothers Day" led on revenue ($5.1K) with a real incentive (free shipping + tote, code FORMOM26) — and no image needed. A clear offer beats a pretty picture on SMS.
  • Specific, in-season product won clicks. Seersucker and Swim posted the best click rates (1.6%), each pairing a named seasonal category with a clean MMS shot.
  • Tighter targeting pays. The Swim send went to the smallest, most-filtered list (6.2K, unengaged suppressed) and returned the highest revenue per recipient at $0.46.
  • Click-through is the gap. Rates ran 0.7–1.6% vs the ~3% benchmark — shorten copy, lead with the offer or the single best product, and keep one clear link.
