May was a high-cadence month built around two pillars: a Mother's Day full-list push in the first week and a steady drumbeat of men's product-category drops (seersucker, linen, pants, swim, denim, polos, pullovers) through the rest of the month. Flows quietly out-earned campaigns, generating 56% of total attributed revenue from just 38K recipients — a reminder that automated, high-intent touchpoints remain the most efficient revenue per send. Campaign volume was heavy (1.73M total recipients) but broadcast-style, so revenue-per-recipient stayed low while reach and top-of-funnel engagement stayed strong.

Strategy: what went out

The campaign calendar followed a clear segmented-merchandising playbook. Early May leaned into Mother's Day with four full-list "Edit" sends plus an SMS push, then pivoted almost entirely to men's category storytelling — each send targeting a product-affinity segment (Men's Pant Affinity, Polos Affinity, Short Sleeve Shirt Affinity, Denim Affinity) layered on top of a 180-day engaged-email base. Women's sends went to predicted-female and women's-shopper segments. Nearly every send excluded recent purchasers (7 & 30 day), bounce-risk subscribers, and SMS error profiles — disciplined list hygiene that protects deliverability.

Takeaways — Campaigns

  • Men's drives the business. 15 men's email sends produced ~$92.6K (74% of email campaign revenue). Continue the category-drop cadence and protect this engine.
  • Best individual performers were men's bottoms and swim: Short Sleeve Shirts ($11.1K), Swim Edit 02 ($9.8K), and Generation Pants ($8.4K) led the month.
  • SMS punches above its weight. At $0.23 revenue/recipient vs $0.08 for email, reserve SMS for marquee launches and time-sensitive offers rather than broad sends.
  • Mother's Day full-list sends delivered reach and strong opens (46–47%) but low click/conversion — broad "edit" emails work better as awareness than as direct-response. Consider tighter product focus or a clearer offer next year.
  • Women's is small but growing in engagement ( ~$10.8K). Continue with caddence and focus new key women's categories to keep growing the engaged female segment.
  • Why the low performers lagged — it's product & audience, not timing. The weakest men's send, Pullovers ($2.9K), still opened at 58% but barely converted — a warm-layer product landing in late-May warm-weather demand while swim, shorts and pants were winning. Time edits with seasonally appropriate items to grow conversion. Women's sends cap lower mainly on audience size (a ~33–48K engaged-female base vs ~60–84K for men's), not creative. Day-of-week showed no clear penalty — the two Sunday-noon sends (Generation Pants and SWIM) were both top-five performers. The softest subject lines were the generic full-list ones ("Gifts for Mom," "Happy Mother's Day") rather than the product-named men's drops.

Full-month view — patterns across all 40 sends

  • Tighter segments open ~10 points higher. Sends to affinity / 180-day-engaged segments opened 56–63%, while full-list sends (the Mother's Day edits, Resort, Best Sellers, full SWIM) opened 46–49%. The engaged-segment targeting on the men's drops is the single biggest engagement lever — and explains most of the open-rate spread across the month.
  • Heavy cadence, healthy list. May ran ~34 email + 6 SMS sends (more than one a day), yet unsubscribe rate held at ~0.10% and delivery at 99.6%. The disciplined exclusions on nearly every send — recent purchasers (7 & 30 day), bounce-risk and SMS-error profiles — protected the base despite the volume.
  • The pant/denim affinity segment did the heavy lifting — and the most sends. Six pant/denim drops hit that segment in May (5-Pocket, Infinity, Zuma, Generation Pants, Generation Denim, Union Denim). Performance held, but it's the first segment to watch for fatigue — space these out or rotate categories.
  • Resends are working as a second touch. The Seersucker resend to non-openers added ~$4.7K, and the women's Shorts AM→PM split recovered extra orders. Both reused the original subject, though — varying the subject line on the second touch is the easy win left on the table.
  • Weekends convert. The Saturday and Sunday-noon sends ranked among the month's strongest, so the calendar should treat weekends as prime real estate for some of our strongest content pieces.

Takeaways — Flows

  • Welcome flows are the crown jewel. Welcome SMS ($62.8K) and Welcome Series Email ($39.4K) together drove $102K — over half of flow revenue. Keep new-subscriber capture funded and well-supplied with offers.
  • Abandonment recovery is healthy. Add-to-Cart, Browse and Checkout flows combined for ~$48K; the Add to Cart flow's $411 average order value is the highest in the account.
  • Loyalty flows are emerging winners. Loyalty Welcome ($9.5K) and Points Earned ($5.6K) convert well — worth expanding loyalty-triggered automation.
  • Zero-revenue flows to review: Loyalty Points Reminder and Wishlist drove engagement but $0 attributed revenue in May — revisit offer, timing, or product links.
  • Highest-unsub flow: Next Best Product – Cross Sell (48 unsubs) and Welcome Series (175) — monitor frequency and content to protect the list.

New marketable profiles in May

Both lists grew on a net basis in May. Email added 3,374 new subscribers against 2,162 opt-outs — a healthy 1.56 : 1 acquisition-to-loss ratio. New-subscriber volume spiked in the first full week (Mother's Day traffic) then settled into a steady ~550–650/week baseline. SMS growth was slower (1.32 : 1) and unsubscribes rose in the back half of the month, coinciding with the heavier late-May SMS send cadence.

Takeaways — Subscribers

  • Acquisition is outpacing churn on both channels — the foundation of compounding owned-audience revenue.
  • Email churn is concentrated in flows & high-frequency sends. Welcome Series (175 unsubs) and broad full-list sends carry most opt-outs; tightening segmentation can preserve growth.
  • SMS unsubs climbed in weeks 4–5 alongside more frequent texting. Cap SMS frequency and keep suppressing recent purchasers / unengaged to protect the smaller, higher-value list.
  • Capitalize on the Mother's Day acquisition spike — the early-May cohort (1,380 email subs in one week) should be flowing through the Welcome series; ensure that handoff is well-tuned given Welcome's outsized revenue.

Why this won best creative

  • A specific, benefit-clear subject. "Clean Classic Shirts" sets a plain, confident expectation — nothing to decode — paired with a preview ("New men's short sleeves, in now.") that confirms the offer.
  • Shoppable from the first scroll. It opens on a clean single-product hero, not a lifestyle or text banner — the product is the message, so intent-to-buy carries straight through.
  • Right product, right segment, right time. An in-season staple (short sleeves into summer) sent to the engaged men's base — the result was the month's best efficiency at $0.18/recipient and the top revenue overall ($11.1K).

Overall, May was a strong, disciplined creative month. The team kept a consistent, clean brand look — single-product hero shots on white, a steady logo lockup, and tidy preview text — and let merchandising do the work. Performance separated cleanly along two lines: tightly-targeted men's product drops with specific, product-named subject lines were the clear winners, while broad full-list Mother's Day sends drove reach and opens but converted less efficiently. The variance came from audience and specificity, not design quality.

Two findings sharpen that read. First, opens are inflated by Apple Mail Privacy, so the real gap is click-through — the creative reliably earns the open; turning that open into a click-to-buy is where to push (clearer in-email CTAs, fewer competing links). Second, the details decide it: winners led on a shoppable product hero, while the softest sends opened on a text banner ("Happy Mother's Day") or a vague lifestyle shot — and the free-tote offer that could have driven response sat in preview text instead of the subject line. Resends recovered real revenue but reused the original subject; varying it on the second touch is an easy win.

★ Main takeaway for the team

The creative is doing its job — the next gain isn't prettier emails, it's sharper specificity. Name the product in the subject, lead on a shoppable hero, put the offer in the subject line, and carry the men's "name it, show it" discipline into the women's and full-list sends. Then chase the click, not the open.

What worked across subject lines & above-the-fold

  • Specific product names beat generic hooks. The top earners named the product or franchise — "Clean Classic Shirts," "The Pant Series 03: Generation™," "Pool Day, Solved" — while vaguer lines ("It's Heating Up," "Gifts for Mom") under-indexed.
  • Lead with the product, not a lifestyle banner. Every top performer opened on a clean, single-product hero shot above the fold; softer editorial/lifestyle openers (Resort, the Mother's Day edits) converted less efficiently.
  • Preview text that adds a concrete detail outperforms. "New men's short sleeves, in now." and "New swim trunks, plus the soft tees and tanks to match." reinforce the offer; generic previews ("Best sellers, all week.") leave the open-decision to the subject alone.
  • Offer-driven Mother's Day subjects ("Last Call for Mom," "Free tote on orders $300+") were the strongest of the full-list sends — urgency + incentive lifted an otherwise broad audience.
Audience:

SMS was the month's most efficient channel — ~$0.23 revenue per recipient, roughly 3× email — built on short, link-first messages and (mostly) a single MMS product image. Click-through is the headroom: rates ran 0.7–1.6%, below the ~3% benchmark, so the lever is the same as email — a tighter message and a clearer reason to tap. Takeaways — SMS

  • Offer drove the top send. "Mothers Day" led on revenue ($5.1K) with a real incentive (free shipping + tote, code FORMOM26) — and no image needed. A clear offer beats a pretty picture on SMS.
  • Specific, in-season product won clicks. Seersucker and Swim posted the best click rates (1.6%), each pairing a named seasonal category with a clean MMS shot.
  • Tighter targeting pays. The Swim send went to the smallest, most-filtered list (6.2K, unengaged suppressed) and returned the highest revenue per recipient at $0.46.
  • Click-through is the gap. Strong revenue per recipient but sub-benchmark CTR — shorten copy, lead with the offer or the single best product, and keep one clear link.
